Auction: Bayesian Linear Regression

Description:

This data set, "Auction", provides the selling price and age of antique clocks, and the number of bidders in the auction.

Variables:

  • Age - Age of the antique clock.
  • Bidders - The number of bidders in the auction.
  • Price - The final selling price of the clock. 

This example JASP file demonstrates the use of Bayesian linear regression. Specifically, we are interested whether and to what extent the age of the clocks and the number of bidders in the auction predict the final selling price.

Reference:

Mendenhall, W, and Sincich, TL (1993). A Second Course in Statistics: Regression Analysis (6th ed.), New Jersey: Prentice-Hall.

Bayesian Linear Regression

Let's explain the Bayesian linear regression. Each row of the table below represents one model. The Null model contains only the intercept (it is stating that Age nor Bidders predict Price). The Age model and the Bidders model each include the regression coefficient of the corresponding variable. The model Age + Bidders includes both predictors.

The column P(M) shows the prior probability of the respective models. In this case, all models were deemed equally likely before seeing the data. The P(M|Data) and BFM provide the posterior model probabilities and the Bayes factor for model M, respectivelly. The posterior model probabilities express the probability of a model after seeing the data. The Bayes factor quantifies the data-induced change from prior model odds to posterior model odds. The BF01 column indicates the Bayes factors of the models in each row compared to the model in the first row. Therefore the first row always has BF01 = 1 (because the model is compared against itself). Here we selected "compare to best model", which means the the model is the first row is the one that predicted the data best and acts as the "null" model. R2 is the traditional statistic -- the coefficient of determination.

Given we selected the model with two predictors, we can interpret its coefficient estimates. On average, an increase of clock's age by one year corresponds to a rise of the selling price by 12.4 pounds. With one more bidder, the selling price on average increases by 83.5 pounds.

The model with two predictors clearly outperforms all other models (explaining about 89% of the variance), as the data are over 9e7  times more likely to occur under this model than under the second-best model that only includes Age as a predictor. This leads to a posterior model probability that is essentially one. We have obtained an overwhelming evidence that both age of clock and number of bidders in the auction relate to the final selling price. [NB. we have not explored a model that includes an interaction between Age and Bidders]
