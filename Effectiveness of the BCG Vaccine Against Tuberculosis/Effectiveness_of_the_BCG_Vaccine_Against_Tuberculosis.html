Effectiveness of the BCG Vaccine Against Tuberculosis

Description

This data set, "BCG Vaccine Data," summarizes findings from multiple studies examining the efficacy of the Bacillus Calmette–Guérin (BCG) vaccine in preventing tuberculosis. Each study provides information on the number of tuberculosis cases and non-cases in both the vaccinated (treatment) and unvaccinated (control) groups. Additionally, study-level moderators such as latitude and allocation method are included. In this example JASP file, we analyze the BCG data set using meta-analysis and meta-regression techniques to assess the overall vaccine efficacy and explore how latitude and allocation method may moderate this effect.


Variables

trial: trial number

author: author(s)

year: publication year

tpos: number of TB positive cases in the treated (vaccinated) group

tneg: number of TB negative cases in the treated (vaccinated) group

cpos: number of TB positive cases in the control (non-vaccinated) group

cneg: number of TB negative cases in the control (non-vaccinated) group

ablat: absolute latitude of the study location (in degrees)

alloc: method of treatment allocation (random, alternate, or systematic assignment)


The data set also contains the label which is a concatenation of the author and year variable added via the "Add Column" functionality in the JASP data editor (using the R syntax and `paste0(author, " (", year, ")")` command).


The data set, example, and description is based on the documentation of the metadat R package.

Effect Size Computation

First, we compute effect sizes and standard errors from the study summaries included in the table. We select Independent groups design, Binary Measurement, and RR Effect size to compute the log risk ratio (RR) for each estimate. The risk ratio provides a measure of the vaccine's efficacy by comparing the risk of developing tuberculosis between the vaccinated and unvaccinated groups.

Funnel Plot

Second, we generate a funnel plot to visualize the distribution of the computed effect sizes against their standard errors. The plot can be helpful in multiple ways. It can help identify issues with the data (e.g., outliers resulting from incorrectly extracted information) or help in assessing potential publication bias or heterogeneity among studies (however, it is important to mention that the funnel plot is not considered a reliable method for identifying the presence or absence of publication bias).

Funnel Plot

We select the Funnel under H1 option which centers the funnel plot on the meta-analytic estimate of the mean effect. The individual lines show the prediction interval (0.90, 0.95, 0.99). While the majority of the estimates lie within the 99% prediction interval, there are more studies than expected outside of the interval indicating the presence of additional heterogeneity. The funnel plot also appeared symmetrical (i.e., it does not indicate the presence of publication bias) and it also does not indicate any outliers. Finally, the Power enchancement option adds a background of vertical stripes that allows us the assess the power of the estimates to detect the meta-analytic mean effect. Here we see that the data set contains both high-powered and low-powered studies to detect the meta-analytic mean effect.

Funnel Plot Asymmetry Tests

We can also formally test the null hypothesis of no funnel plot asymmetry using the Funnel plot asymmetry tests option. Funnel plot asymmetry can indicate potential publication bias or other small-study effects that may influence the results of the meta-analysis. In this example, we focus on the Meta-regression test, which assesses asymmetry by including the standard error as a predictor in the meta-analytic model. The first two columns show the test statistic and the p-value which shows that the test fails to reject the null hypothesis of no funnel assymetry. The last three columns provide the Limit Estimate, i.e., meta-analytic estimate adjusted for the asymmetry (in the limit of standard error equal to zero).

Classical Meta-Analysis

Third, we perform a meta-regression to estimate the overall effect of the BCG vaccine on preventing tuberculosis and examine whether the effect is moderated by the allocation method and latitude. We also generate additional visualizations to summarize the findings.

The Meta-Analytic Tests table contains hypothesis tests concerning the overall model. The test of Residual Heterogeneity tests the null hypothesis of no between-study heterogeneity (after accounting for the predictors). The test of Pooled effect tests the null hypothesis of no pooled effect. The test of Moderation tests the omnibus null hypothesis of no moderation by any of the included predictors (included in the model in the Predictors variable input and further specified in the Model Section).


We find that including both moderators simultaneously does not allow us to reject the null hypothesis of no moderation on the usual α = 0.05 level. However, we proceed with examining the model and the moderation effect further as the included 13 estimates are most likely underpowered to detect meaningful moderation effects.

Estimated Marginal Means and Contrasts Summary

The Estimated Marginal Means allow us to further examine the effect of moderators by summarizing the predicted effect at each moderator level. Here, we compute the predicted effect for the allocation moderator and find that the largest effect would be at the random allocation level. However, a pairwise comparison of the estimated marginal mean via the Add contrasts option reveals no statistically significant differences between the allocation levels.


We can also obtain the Adjusted Estimate by selecting the Add adjusted estimate checkbox. Importantly, this estimate differs from the Pooled Effect Size Estimate; whereas the Adjusted Estimate provides the effect size averaged across all the predictors (i.e, it weights the individual factor levels equally) the Pooled Effect Size Estimate provides the effect size averaged across the observed estimates (i.e., it weights the individual factor levels proportionally to their occurence in the data set).

Forest Plot

The Forest Plot section allows you to specify a highly customizable forest plot. Here, we include several variables in the Study information panel to be displayed in the left side of the figure. We add titles, adjust the column width, and modify the study order. In the Estimated marginal means panel we include the estimated marginal means for the allocation moderator which allows us to include both the moderation information and the by-level estimated marginal means in the figure. In the Model information panel we include the summary information and the pooled effect size estimate. Lastly, in the Settings panel we adjust the relative size of the figure, add a vertical line at zero, and include color mapping of the effect size estimates based on the allocation variable. Each of these panels can be hidden/shown independently of the remaining panels allowing for a wide range of visualizations.

Bubble Plots

The Bubble Plot allows us to visualize the predicted effect of a moderator alongside the effect size estimates (try changing the Selected Variable to alloc to explore the visualization for the categorical moderator). Here we see that the effectiveness of the BCG vaccine is increasing with increasing latitude, with all effect size estimates well aligned with the meta-regression prediction.

In summary, we conducted a meta-analysis to evaluate the efficacy of the BCG vaccine in preventing tuberculosis. First, we calculated the effect sizes and standard errors by computing the log risk ratios (RR) for each study, providing a quantitative measure of vaccine effectiveness. Next, we generated a funnel plot to visualize the distribution of these effect sizes against their standard errors. The symmetrical appearance of the funnel plot suggested the absence of significant publication bias and revealed no outliers, although some heterogeneity among studies was observed. Finally, we performed a meta-regression analysis to estimate the overall effect and examine potential moderation by allocation method and latitude. While the inclusion of both moderators did not yield statistically significant moderation effects at the conventional α = 0.05 level, likely due to limited power from the small sample size, visualizations such as the forest plot and bubble plot indicated that vaccine effectiveness increases with latitude. The allocation method did not significantly influence the effect size estimates. These analyses provide valuable insights into factors that may impact the BCG vaccine's efficacy across different study settings.
