Effects of Modified School Calendars on Student Achievement

Description

This data set, "Studies on the Effects of Modified School Calendars on Student Achievement," contains results from 56 studies examining the impact of modified school calendars on student achievement. Traditionally, schools follow a calendar with a long summer break, but some have switched to a modified calendar with more frequent, shorter breaks throughout the year while keeping the total number of school days approximately the same. The data have a multilevel structure, with schools nested within districts.

In this example JASP file, we perform a multilevel meta-analysis to estimate the overall effect of modified school calendars on student achievement and to partition the heterogeneity at different levels (districts and schools within districts). We also explore the profile likelihoods of the variance components to assess the stability of the estimates.


Variables

district: district id number

school: school id number (within district)

study: study id number

yi: standardized mean difference

vi: corresponding sampling variance

year: year of the study


We add the se variable corresponding to the standard errors of the effect size estimates using the "Add Column" functionality in the JASP data editor (using the square root of the vi variable).


The data set, example, and description is based on the documentation of the metadat R package.

https://cran.r-project.org/package=metadat

Classical Meta-Analysis (Multilevel/Multivariate)

We fit a multilevel random-effects model to account for the hierarchical structure of the data. The model includes random effects at the district and school levels to capture between-district and between-school heterogeneity. We specify the model with random intercepts at the district and school levels by using the Nested (multilevel) Structure Type in the Random Effects / Model Structure Section.

Profile Likelihood Plot

The Profile Likelihood Plots allow us to evaluate the uncertainty around the variance component estimates. The first figure corresponds to the between-district variance and the second figure corresponds to the between-school variance. Both profile likelihood plots show the log-likelihood values as a function of different values of the variance component. We see the the progile likelihoods are quite peaked, indicating well indentidied heterogeneity components.

The analysis indicates that adopting a modified school calendar can positively impact student achievement. However, the effectiveness varies across districts and schools, highlighting the importance of considering local factors when implementing such calendars. Further research could explore the specific factors contributing to the heterogeneity to better understand how to maximize the benefits of modified school calendars.
