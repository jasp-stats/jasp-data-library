El Nino: Circular Statistics

Description:

This fictional data set, "El Nino", provides wind direction measurements in Hawaii during the months August, July, and September. Created by Aaron Bahde at the University of Tuebingen (2019) under the CC-BY > 4.0 license.

Variables:

  1. WindDirection - The wind direction measured in Degrees.
  2. Month - The month in which the wind direction was measured.

In this example JASP file, we analyse whether the wind direction in Hawaii changed during these three months. A significant change usually happens during the climate phenomenon "El Nino". We will plot this directional data set and perform a circular one-way ANOVA to show exactly this significant change.

References: 

El Nino-Southern Oscillation outlook, Australian Bureau of Meteorlogy, http://www.bom.gov.au/climate/enso (February, 2019)

Fisher, N.I. (1993). Statistical Analysis of Circular Data. Chapter 2, Chapter 5. Cambridge University Press.

Circular Descriptives Plots

Looking at the circular diagrams of the data, we first note the black line that indicates the mean vector. It points in the mean direction and it's length is a measure for the concentration of the data around it. 

We see that there is a strong indication in July that the mean wind direction is around 260° (the mean vector is long). However, for August we see a shorter mean vector and the measurements do not show a strong trend. Finally, for September we find a strong tendency of the wind to blow in the direction of around 150°.

We may conclude here that the wind direction changed gradually from July to September. To statistically justify this observation, we now run a circular one-way ANOVA.

Circular Multiple-Sample Tests

The Watson-Wheeler test (which is a non-parametric one-way ANOVA on circular data) indeed shows a siginifant result of the factor Month on the wind directions in Hawaii.

Visualization of directional data with circular diagrams can help to get a first impression of the problem. Especially the meaning of the mean direction can be evaluated in more detail. Here, we found that the mean wind directions in Hawaii gradually changed from 270° in July to 150° in September.

A circular one-way ANOVA showed a significant effect of the month in which the wind was measured.
