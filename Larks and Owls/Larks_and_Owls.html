Larks and Owls: Classical Generalized Linear Mixed Models

Description:

This data set, "Larks and Owls", provides measures of Mind Wandering (MW) obtained via Sustained Attention to Response Task and Chronotype obtained via Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire. All participants (n = 130) completed the Sustained Attention Task twice: once in the morning and once in the evening while reporting the amount of sleep on the previous night. Subset of the data reported by Van Opstal, Aslanov, and Schnelzer (2021).

Variables:

  1. Chronotype - Chronotype classification based on the Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire score (Morning/Intermediate/Evening).
  2. TimeOfDay - Time of completion of the Sustained Attention to Response Task (Morning: 8AM, Evening: 8.30PM).
  3. Sleep - Amount of sleep on the previous night (in hours).
  4. MWCount - Number of times participants responded as "Off-task" in the Sustained Attention to Response Task.
  5. Subject - Number identifying participants across the repeated measures.
  6. Age - Participants' age.
  7. Gender - Participants' gender.
  8. MEQScore - Participants score on the Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire.

This example JASP file demonstrates the use of the frequentist versions of generalized linear mixed effect models. Specifically, we will test whether mind wandering depends on participants' chronotype and whether the effect is modified by the time when the task is administered.

References:

Van Opstal, F., Aslanov, V., and Schnelzer, S. (2021). Mind-wandering in larks and owls: The effects of chronotype and time of day on the frequency of task-unrelated thoughts. Preprint at: https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/pqkc2

Generalized Linear Mixed Models

To perform the analysis, we first need to specify the model type by setting the parametric Family: Poisson (since our response is a count variable) and selecting the analysis variables. We select the Dependent variable (MWCount, describing the number of times participants mind-wandered), Fixed effects variables (Chronotype, TimeOfDay, and Sleep, specifying the predictors), and the Random effects grouping factors (Subject, defining the repeated measures within participants).

Second, we open the model tab and remove the three-way interaction and all two-way interactions but Chronotype * TimeOfDay as they are not relevant to the hypotheses. A note under the ANOVA Summary table notifies us that the (default) Random effects structure is overspecified. Indeed, the model was defined to estimate random effects of Chronotype -- which does not vary across subjects, and TimeOfDay which with one observation per subject does not allow us to estimate random slopes. We remove the warnings by unselecting the corresponding random slopes, however, we are still presented with Numerical problems warning indicating that the specified model (with a random slope of sleep) is still too complex to estimate from the data. We proceed by removing the last remaining random slope. It unfortunately does not resolve the warning, however, there are no more random effects to remove from the model. Therefore, we draw inference from the resulting, random intercept only model, while keeping in mind that the results might be anticonservatives, i.e., we might expirience experience an inflated error rate.

The ANOVA Summary table informs us about statistically significant effects of Sleep, Chronotype, and the interaction between Chronotype and TimeOfDay. These omnibus Chi-square tests tell us that at least one of the Chronotype levels is statistically different from the remaining ones, however, it does not tell us which one and what is the direction of the difference (similarly to the Chronotype and TimeOfDay interaction). To figure out the statistically significant pattern, we create a figure that visualizes mean estimates for the individual groups in the Plots section.

(Note that eyeballing of Fixed effects estimates, available under the Options section is not helpful since JASP uses sum contrast coding which does not directly map on the differences between groups.)

Plot

We obtain the mind wandering model based mean estimates (y-axis, as points with 95% CI) for each individual Chronotype (different lines) and TimeOfDay (x-axis) by selecting the corresponding variables into the corresponding boxes. The Background data show option determines which Random effects grouping factor is used for aggregating the data visualized in the background of the figure. In our case, Subjects, the only possible option, determines that each point visualized in the figure corresponds to a Subject estimate for a given combination of TimeOfDay and Chronotype conditions. We further differentiate the factors by both the Color and Shape and draw a legend on the RIght side of the figure.

We see that the Morning chronotype participants mind wander less when they complete the task in Morning in contrast to Evening chronotype participants. The Intermediate and Evening chronotype participants show the opposite patern, however, with smaller differences between the task completion times.

Now we obtain the mean mind wandering estimates for the combination of each of the Chronotype and TimeOfDay levels by moving the corresponding variables into the Selected variables box. The resulting estimated marginal mean are not simple means of each subgroup -- the estimated marginal means are based on the generalized mixed effect model and account for the Random effects grouping factors and are adjusted for the remaining Fixed effects. The checked Response scale option defines that the estimates are already transformed through the link function to the outcome scale, i.e., counts (unchecking it would result in estimates on the linear scale, pre-log-transformed, for the rate parameter of the Poisson family.

Finally, we perform statistical significance tests for differences between some of the subgroups with Contrasts. Each contrast defines a comparison of the set of estimated marginal means. The contrasts are specified via the contrasts specification matrix. The first column provides mapping to the Estimated Marginal Means table, with the following two columns reiterating the corresponding levels. The individual contrasts are then specified in subsequent columns which can be added by clicking the Add Contrast button.

We have to specify numerical values in the contrast columns which define how is the comparison between the Estimated Marginal Means computed. The Estimated Marginal Means are multiplied by the corresponding Contrast values and summed. Consequently, setting 1 to one of the groups and -1 to another group results in difference between these two groups. More complex contrasts can be set up, e.g., an interaction can be computed as a difference between two differences etc.

Here, we specify and test the following contrasts:

  1. Contrast 1: The effect of task completion time on mind wandering for the Morning chronotype participants (i.e., the difference between the estimated mind wandering in the Evening and Morning task completion in the Morning chronotype group).
  2. Contrast 2: The effect of task completion time on mind wandering for the Intermediate chronotype participants (i.e., the difference between the estimated mind wandering in the Evening and Morning task completion in the Intermediate chronotype group).
  3. Contrast 3: The effect of task completion time on mind wandering for the Evening chronotype participants (i.e., the difference between the estimated mind wandering in the Evening and Morning task completion in the Evening chronotype group).
  4. Contrast 4: The difference in mind wandering between the participants with the Evening vs Intermediate chronotype combined across the time completion task (i.e., the difference between the mind wandering of the Evening chronotype participants in both the Morning and Evening time of task completion and the mind wandering of the Intermediate chronotype participants in both the Morning and Evening time of task completion).
  5. Contrast 5: The difference in mind wandering between the participants with the Evening vs Morning chronotype combined across the time completion task (i.e., the difference between the mind wandering of the Evening chronotype participants in both the Morning and Evening time of task completion and the mind wandering of the Morning chronotype participants in both the Morning and Evening time of task completion).
  6. Contrast 6. The difference of the effect of task completion time between the Intermediate and Morning chronotype participants (i.e., the difference of Contrast 2 and Contrast 1).
  7. Contrast 7. The difference of the effect of task completion time between the Evening and Morning chronotype participants (i.e., the difference of Contrast 3 and Contrast 1).

We find statistically significant effects of time completion on mind wandering time for Morning and Evening chronotype participants (contrasts 1 and 3) with the Morning chronotype participants tending to mind wander more in the evening and Evening chronotype participants tending to mindwander more in the morning. Furtheremore, we find statistical significant differences of mind wandering between Evening and Intermediate and Evening and Morning chronotype participants when combining both the Morning and Evening taks completion times (contrasts 4 and 5) with the Evening chronotype participants mind wandering the most. Finally, we find statistically siginificant differences between the effect of task completion on mind wandering amongst the Morning and Intermediate as well as amongst the Morning and Evening chronotype participants (contrasts 6 and 7), suggesting that the task completion time affects the Morning chronotype differently than the rest.
