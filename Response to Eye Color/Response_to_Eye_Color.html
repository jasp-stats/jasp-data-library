Response to Eye Color: One-Way ANOVA

Description:

This data set, "Response to Eye Color", provides post-advertisement attitudes towards a brand expressed by four different groups - each group saw the same advertisement except for the aspect that was manipulated: the eye-color of the model.

Variables:

  • Group - Experimental conditions (`Blue' = Model with blue eyes, `Brown' = Model with brown eyes, `Green'= Model with green eyes, `Down' = Model's eye color cannot be seen).
  • Subj - Participant number.
  • Score - An average of 10 survey questions about attitudes towards the brand (7-point Likert scale). Higher averages correspond to more positive attitudes.

This example JASP file demonstrates the use of one-way ANOVA. Specifically, we assess the adequacy of the null hypothesis that the attitudes are the same regardless of the eye-color of the model

References:

Moore, D. S., McCabe, G. P., and Craig, B. A. (2012). Introduction to the Practice of Statistics (7th ed.). New York: Freeman.

Simpson, P. M., Sturges, D. L., and Tanguma, J. (2008). The eyes have it, or do they? The effects of model eye color and eye gaze on consumer as response. The Journal of Applied Business and Economics, 8: 60-72.

* Vovk-Sellke Maximum p -Ratio: Based on the p -value, the maximum possible odds in favor of H₁ over H₀ equals 1/(-e p log(p )) for p ≤ .37 (Sellke, Bayarri, & Berger, 2001).

The results suggest some differences between the groups, although the overall effect is somewhat small, so we should expect only minor differences between the groups. VS-MPR shows that the evidence for the alternative hypothesis (the groups differ from each other) is at best about 3 in favor compared to the null hypothesis; a ratio which shows that the evidence is not very strong.

Assumption Checks

Assumption of ANOVA (Levene's test, followed by normal Q-Q plot). Levene's test is not significant (and the differences of variances are very small - see the descriptives), but the Q-Q plot of residuals suggests a slight violation of normality. It is possible to conduct a non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test to see whether the conclusions from ANOVA change.
