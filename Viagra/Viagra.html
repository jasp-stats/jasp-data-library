Viagra: ANCOVA

Description:

This fictional data set, "Viagra", provides men's libido (and that of their partners) after having been administered different doses of viagra.

Variables: 

  • dose - Viagra dosage administered (Placebo, Low Dose, High Dose).
  • lidibo - Participant's libido (measured over the course of one week).
  • partnerLibido - Libido of participant's partner (measured over the course of one week).

This example JASP file demonstrates the use of ANCOVA. We will investigate whether different doses of viagra affect a person's libido. The partner's libido can be taken into account as a covariate.

Reference:

Field, A. P. (2013). Discovering Statistics Using IBM SPSS Statistics (4th ed.). London: Sage. [Fictional data set]

ANOVA

jaspAnova::Anova(
        version = "0.18",
        formula = partnerLibido ~ dose,
        contrasts = list(list(contrast = "none", variable = "dose")),
        descriptivePlotErrorBar = TRUE,
        descriptivePlotHorizontalAxis = "dose",
        descriptives = TRUE,
        plotWidthDescriptivesPlotLegend = 450)

First we conduct an univariate ANOVA to assess whether experimental groups differ on the covariate (the interpretation of the subsequent ANCOVA is much more straightforward when they do not). The results suggest that we cannot reject the hypothesis that the groups do not differ on the covariate.

ANCOVA

jaspAnova::Ancova(
        version = "0.18",
        formula = libido ~ partnerLibido + dose,
        covariates = "partnerLibido",
        contrasts = list(list(contrast = "simple", variable = "dose")),
        descriptivePlotErrorBar = TRUE,
        descriptivePlotHorizontalAxis = "dose",
        descriptives = TRUE,
        effectSizeEstimates = TRUE,
        effectSizePartialEtaSquared = TRUE,
        homogeneityTests = TRUE,
        marginalMeanCiCorrection = "sidak",
        marginalMeanComparedToZero = TRUE,
        marginalMeanTerms = ~ dose,
        plotWidthDescriptivesPlotLegend = 450,
        postHocTypeStandardEffectSize = TRUE,
        qqPlot = TRUE)

So what happens when the variance associated with the partner's Libido is accounted for? Suddenly the effect of dose becomes statistically significant at the .05 level (i.e., p = 0.027).
