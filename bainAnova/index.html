Bain ANOVA

This is an example of a bain-anova with dependent variable "postnumb" (a child's number knowledge score after watching Sesame Street for one year) and factor "site" of origin of the child (site1 = disadvantaged inner city, site2 = advantaged suburban, site3 = advantaged rural, site4 = disadvantaged rural; site5 = Spanish speaking). 

Hypothesis Legend
  Hypothesis
H1 site1=site2=site3=site4=site5
H2 site3<site4<site1<site5<site2

Two hypotheses are being evaluated. H1 states that all five means are equal. H2 states that the knowledge of numbers increases going from site3 to site4 to site1 to site5 to site2.

Bain ANOVA

Hu 0.014

Note.  BF.u denotes the Bayes factor of the hypothesis at hand versus the unconstrained hypothesis Hu. BF.c denotes the Bayes factor of the hypothesis at hand versus its complement. PMPa contains the posterior model probabilities of the hypotheses specified. PMPb adds Hu, the unconstrained hypothesis. PMPc adds Hc, the complement of the union of the hypotheses specified. All PMPs are based on equal prior model probabilities.

BF1c = 0, that is, there is no support for H1 in the data. BF2c = 177.18, that is, the support in the data for H2 is 177.18 times larger than the support for its complement "not H2".

PMP c shows the support in the data for H1, H2, and Hc (the joint complement of H1 and H2, that is, "not H1 or H2"). As can be seen, the probability that H1 is the best of these three hypotheses is .994. This implies that a preference for H2 comes with a Bayesian error probability of .000 + .006 = .006.

This matrix presents the relative support in the data BF21 for H2 and H1. As can be seen , the support for H2 is 6 x e+12 times (a huge number) larger than the support for H1.

For each level of site the sample size, mean, standard deviation, standard error and credible interval for the mean are presented. As can be seen, the order of the means in the data corresponds to the order of the means in H2. This is the main explanation why the data prefer H2 over H1 and Hu.

Posterior Probabilities

Descriptives Plot

The first plot shows a visual representation of the size of the posterior probabilities both without (on the left) and with (on the right) the inclusion of Hc. It can immediately be seen that in both cases the data prefer H2, that is, H2 is a better hypothesis than H1, and H2 is a good hypothesis because it is more supported than Hc. In the middle plot Hc is replaced by Hu. This plot was the predecessor of the plot on the right and should no longer be used.

The second plot shows a visual representation of the descriptive statistics. As can be seen, the order of the means in the data corresponds to the order of the means in H2. This is the main explanation why the data prefer H2 over H1 and Hu.
